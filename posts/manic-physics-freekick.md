## The showcase: Roberto Carlos' 1997 free kick

The most famous banana kick ever — aimed metres wide of the goal, curling back in past a wall of defenders. Let's build it. Not a drawing of it. *It.*

### Beat 1 — One line of real physics

```manic
// speed 30 m/s, aimed 22° right, heavy sidespin
freekick(rc, (kx, ky), 30, 22, 3.1);
color(rc, gold); glow(rc, 0.8); untraced(rc);
```

That single `freekick(...)` runs an **RK4 integration** of the ball's flight at build time: aerodynamic drag *plus* a Magnus side-force proportional to the spin and perpendicular to the velocity — the exact `a = κ (ω̂ × v)` that bends a spun ball. It lays down the gold trajectory and a ball you can fly along it:

```manic
draw(rc, 2.0);   // trace the computed banana
run(rc, 2.0);    // the ball flies the trajectory in real time
```

The ball swerves wide of the wall and curls in by the post — because the solver says it does. Change `spin` to `4.0` and it curls harder. Change it to `0` and it doesn't curl at all. You're not editing a path; you're setting a parameter and watching physics answer.

### Beat 2 — You can *prove* it, because it's real

This is the move a template can never make. Take the **exact same strike** — same speed, same 22° aim — and turn the spin off:

```manic
freekick(ns, (kx, ky), 30, 22, 0);   // no spin, everything else identical
color(ns, red); dashed(ns);
```

The no-spin ball sails **wide right and misses** — a dashed red line straight past the post, while the gold one curls into the goal. That side-by-side is the entire argument for the Magnus effect, and it's airtight *because you didn't stage it.* You changed one number from `3.1` to `0`. The divergence is the physics disagreeing with itself. No commenter can argue with a controlled experiment they watched run.

### Beat 3 — Then explain the *why*, also computed

Here's where the physics kit stops being a stunt and becomes teaching. Rewind the shot, and let the ball *become* a spinning cylinder in an airflow. Streamlines aren't decoration here — they're the integral curves of the flow's velocity field, so the same idea that solved the trajectory solves the flow:

```manic
// each streamline = a path traced through the velocity field
// (uniform flow + a free vortex for the spin), integrated live
trajectory(fl,
  "1 - 1.44*(x*x-y*y)/((x*x+y*y)^2) - 0.7*y/(x*x+y*y)",   // u(x,y)
  "0 - 2.88*x*y/((x*x+y*y)^2) + 0.7*x/(x*x+y*y)",          // v(x,y)
  seed, center, scale);
```

The streamlines **bunch above** the ball (faster flow, lower pressure) and **spread below** (slower, higher pressure). That pressure difference is the sideways push — the Magnus force — that curled the free kick. The visual explanation of *why* is generated by the same engine, from the same first principles, as the shot itself. Mechanism proven, then mechanism explained, and both are true.
